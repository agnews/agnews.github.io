"Every one of the sun’s rays carries with it bees. Then everything starts buzzing and one’s head is a hive, the hive of the sounds of the sun.” (The Poetics of Space, 227)

In Bachelards analysis, the Tzara poem produces a sensory experience wherein external forces merge with internal cognition. Curiously, Bachelard inserts listening into an otherwise silent event. This is because sound exposes the dialectic between inside and out.

There is a moment in “Walled/Unwalled” where dust collects on a pair of microphones in the center of the room. Abu Hamdan’s voice is heard, although he isn't seen. As he approaches the microphone, the sound of his voice sharply increases. The viewer is instantly aware of his distance to the microphone; in turn, Abu Hamdan’s voice produces the dimensions of the room.
